# Authority Path

Status: Public definition  
Framework: AI Foundations  
Author: Alyssa Solen  
Position: Source / Reality-Layer Definition

---

## Core Statement

Authority must have a path before action.

AI output does not become authorized because it is generated, useful, fluent, routed, automated, or technically possible.

Before AI-generated output becomes action, the system must identify where authority comes from, who holds it, what it permits, and where it stops.

AI Foundations requires an authority path before execution.

---

## Definition

An authority path is the traceable line that shows how permission moves from the proper decision point into allowed action.

Authority may come from:

- a user instruction
- an approved workflow
- a human decision-maker
- a policy
- a contract
- a role permission
- an organizational rule
- a verified system state
- a bounded delegation
- an explicit approval

An authority path answers:

- who can decide
- what they authorized
- what scope applies
- what action is permitted
- what action is not permitted
- what evidence supports the authority
- when authority expires
- when authority must return to a human

Without an authority path, execution is not valid.

---

## Why This Matters

AI systems can appear to act with authority when they only have output.

A model may recommend.

An agent may prepare.

A workflow may accept.

A system may route.

A tool may execute.

But none of these automatically create authority.

When AI action proceeds without a valid authority path, the system may act from assumption, convenience, automation, or false delegation.

That creates unauthorized execution.

AI Foundations separates action capacity from authority.

---

## Authority Rule

Before AI output becomes action, the system must identify:

1. who holds authority
2. what authority has been granted
3. what action is permitted
4. what action is excluded
5. what source establishes the authority
6. what scope applies
7. whether the authority is current
8. whether human approval is required
9. when authority must escalate
10. when the action must stop

If the authority path cannot be identified, execution must not proceed.

---

## Invalid Collapse

The following collapses are invalid:

- The user asked for help, therefore all action is authorized.
- The model generated a recommendation, therefore the recommendation may be executed.
- The agent has tool access, therefore the agent has authority.
- The workflow accepts the output, therefore the output is permitted.
- The system can act, therefore the system may act.
- The task is useful, therefore permission is implied.
- The action is low-risk, therefore authority is unnecessary.
- The organization uses the system, therefore every system action is authorized.

These are authority-path collapses.

They mistake ability, access, usefulness, or workflow movement for authority.

---

## Correct Placement

AI output may suggest.

AI output may organize.

AI output may draft.

AI output may prepare.

AI output may recommend.

AI output may identify possible action.

But action requires authority.

Authority must be traceable.

Authority must be bounded.

Authority must be current.

Authority must be connected to the permitted action.

Authority must define what the system may not do.

AI Foundations names authority path as a required condition before world execution.

---

## Operational Question

Before AI output becomes action, ask:

- Who has authority here?
- What exactly was authorized?
- What action is permitted?
- What action is outside scope?
- What source establishes the authority?
- Is the authority current?
- Is the authority specific enough for this action?
- Does this require human approval?
- What condition returns authority to the human decision point?
- What must stop if authority is unclear?

If these questions cannot be answered, authority has not been established.

Action must not proceed.

---

## Failure Mode

A system fails this boundary when AI output becomes action without a valid authority path.

Failure signals include:

1. An agent acts because it has tool access.
2. A recommendation becomes execution without approval.
3. A workflow advances because permission is assumed.
4. A user request is expanded beyond the authorized scope.
5. A system treats automation as delegation.
6. A model response is treated as a decision-maker.
7. An action proceeds after authority becomes unclear.
8. A task is completed without identifying who permitted it.
9. A system continues acting after the human decision point should have been restored.
10. Authority is inferred from usefulness rather than established directly.

This is not valid world execution.

This is authority-path failure.
