# Stop Conditions

Status: Public definition  
Framework: AI Foundations  
Author: Alyssa Solen  
Position: Source / Reality-Layer Definition

---

## Core Statement

AI execution must have stop conditions.

A system must not continue acting simply because it can generate, route, automate, or complete another step.

When authority, proof, state, scope, source, boundary, or consequence fails, action must stop.

AI Foundations requires stop conditions before AI output becomes world execution.

---

## Definition

A stop condition is a defined limit that requires AI-generated action, routing, automation, or workflow continuation to pause, block, return, or escalate.

A stop condition identifies when the system no longer has valid permission to continue.

Stop conditions may be triggered by:

- missing authority
- missing proof
- stale state
- conflicting information
- unclear user intent
- exceeded scope
- insufficient source support
- unknown consequence
- boundary uncertainty
- failed validation
- unsupported confidence
- required human approval
- risk beyond the approved frame

A stop condition prevents execution from continuing after the system has lost the conditions required for valid action.

---

## Why This Matters

AI systems can keep moving.

They can generate another answer.

They can route another task.

They can prepare another document.

They can call another tool.

They can automate another step.

They can infer another missing piece.

They can continue even when the correct action is to stop.

Without stop conditions, AI execution may become unbounded.

The system may keep acting after authority has expired.

It may continue after state becomes stale.

It may proceed after proof breaks.

It may complete a workflow that should have returned to human review.

AI Foundations separates the ability to continue from the right to continue.

---

## Stop Rule

Before AI output becomes world execution, the system must identify:

1. what conditions allow action to proceed
2. what conditions require action to stop
3. what authority must remain valid
4. what proof must remain present
5. what state must remain verified
6. what scope must remain intact
7. what source support must remain connected
8. what consequence must remain understood
9. what uncertainty blocks action
10. who receives control when the stop condition is reached

If stop conditions are not defined, execution is not properly bounded.

---

## Invalid Collapse

The following collapses are invalid:

- The system can continue, therefore it should continue.
- The workflow is incomplete, therefore action should proceed.
- The tool is available, therefore the system may use it.
- The model can infer, therefore the system does not need to stop.
- The task is useful, therefore the boundary can stretch.
- The agent has momentum, therefore review can wait.
- The output is coherent, therefore uncertainty is resolved.
- The system has not failed visibly, therefore it may continue.

These are stop-condition collapses.

They mistake technical continuation for valid execution.

---

## Correct Placement

AI output may proceed when authority remains valid.

AI output may proceed when proof remains sufficient.

AI output may proceed when state remains current.

AI output may proceed when source support remains intact.

AI output may proceed when scope remains bounded.

AI output may proceed when consequence remains understood.

But when a stop condition is reached, action must stop.

Stopping is not system failure.

Stopping is execution control.

Stopping preserves authority.

Stopping preserves source.

Stopping preserves boundary.

Stopping preserves reality-placement.

AI Foundations names stop conditions as required limits on world execution.

---

## Operational Question

Before AI output proceeds into action, ask:

- What would require this action to stop?
- What proof must remain present?
- What authority must remain valid?
- What state must remain current?
- What scope must not be exceeded?
- What source support must remain connected?
- What uncertainty blocks action?
- What consequence requires review?
- Who receives control if the system stops?
- What happens if the stop condition is ignored?

If these questions cannot be answered, stop conditions have not been established.

Execution must not proceed.

---

## Failure Mode

A system fails this boundary when AI execution continues after a stop condition should have been triggered.

Failure signals include:

1. An agent continues after authority expires or becomes unclear.
2. A workflow advances after proof is missing.
3. A system acts from stale or conflicting state.
4. A model fills uncertainty with unsupported inference.
5. A tool is used after scope has been exceeded.
6. Automation continues after source support breaks.
7. A recommendation proceeds after consequence becomes unclear.
8. A task continues without required human approval.
9. A system treats completion as more important than valid boundary.
10. Execution continues because no stop condition was defined.

This is not valid world execution.

This is stop-condition failure.
